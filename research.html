    (PhD theis) Artificial Recurrent Neural Models for Feedback Pathways on Predicting Function in Sensorimotor Integration

    During my PhD I was involved in working with EU FP7-ITN PEOPLE RobotDoC project. The general topic within this project is "Neural Cognition Integration". From this aim, my PhD title is “Artificial Recurrent Neural Models for Feedback Pathways on Predicting Function in Sensorimotor Integration”.

    Neural feedback modulates the low level neural activities via inhibitory or excitatory connections. These feedback pathways exist as neural connections within one level (lateral connections) or between different levels (top-down connections) in the sensorimotor hierarchy. They have a predicting and filtering function in the context of neuronal population coding.

    In this thesis, we propose that the feedback pathways predict and maintain neural activities in biological cognitive systems at each level of the perception and action hierarchy. These activities also perform a predictive sensorimotor integration at most levels of action and perception. We also suggest that the top-most source of the feedback signals may originate from prior knowledge that relates to several cognitive processes, such as expectation, memories and other symbolic representation. These simultaneously emerge with the development of sensorimotor knowledge.
     


    In terms of artificial cognitive systems, we assert that recurrent connections are a basic structure to realise feedback, because they are simple to be implemented and to be trained.
    In the thesis, we present three recurrent neural network models to implement the predictive function in perception or/and action in different artificial cognitive platforms (simulation and humanoid robots). Besides, the three models and their experiments shown in this thesis show that the recurrent neural networks are able to interpret feedback pathways related sensorimotor predictive functions.

    Modelling Two Visual Streams based on Recurrent Connections


    In the first model, we emphasise that the feedback connections facilitate a predictive mechanism to compensate for the neural delay in the two streams (ventral and dorsal streams) of the visual system. We model this with a novel recurrent network with a horizontal product. In the simulation, the recurrent connections give rise to the fast- and slow- changing neural properties in the dorsal- and ventral-like hidden layer.
    Particularly the recurrent connections build a feedback channel to predict the upcoming neural activity in the dorsal-like layer, while another feedback channel maintains stable neural encoding in the ventral-like layer.  This is comparable to the findings in neurobiology.

    Modeling Visual prediction based on Recurrent Connections and CACLA

    In the second part of the thesis, a sensorimotor integration model with visual prediction is implemented, whose visual perception part is considered to be the dorsal stream representation of the first model. It further augments the visual prediction to its role of `vision-for-action'. In the action module of this model, a continuous reinforcement learning algorithm named Continuous Actor-Critic Learning Automaton (CACLA) is applied. Together with the recurrent connections based visual prediction, this model allows a smooth and faster docking behaviour for a humanoid robot.

    Pre-symbolic Emergence from Two Visual Streams
    1. based on Recurrent connections
    we propose that the source of the feedback pathway can be traced in the high-level cognitive processes, such as pre-symbolic representation. Furthermore, the emergence of these cognitive processes and feedback related sensorimotor functions are not independent processes but they also integrate and assist each other in a hierarchical way. Therefore, we augment the model we designed in the first experiment with additional units, called parametric bias (PB) units as a pre-symbolic representation. To examine this horizontal recurrent network with parametric bias (HoRNNPB) model, we design experiments on two robots: one robot is learning while observing the other robot is moving objects. During the learning process of observing sensorimotor primitives, i.e. observing a set of trajectories of arm movements and its colour features, the pre-symbolic representation was self-organized in the parametric units. During prediction, these representational units acted as a prior expectation to guide the robot to recognize and expect various pre-learned sensorimotor primitives.

    2. based on Dynamic Neural Field
    Dynamic Neural Field (DNF) is essentially a continuous recurrent neural network. The recurrent weights in RNN are equivalent to lateral connections in DNF through an interaction kernel and receive external inputs (visual stimuli). This ongoing project aims to model the same principle of language emergence with two stream visual inputs with another way, in which two neural fields encode the “what” and “where” information respectively.
     

    Engineering Projects
    1. Personalized Emotion Recognition from Kinect Sensor
    In this work, a variant version of recurrent neural network is adopted to recognize emotion from human movement.
    From our published paper, we showed that a trained RNNPB can successfully retrieve, recognize and generate different types human body movements based on different emotions . The parametric bias (PB) units in this recurrent network are connected to the hidden layer as ordinary bias units, but the internal values of them are also updated through back-propagation. In this application, the network recognizes which emotion arisen from the behavior sequences. After we trained it with personalized joint movement data obtained from Kinect, in the recognition experiment, the values of PB space were updated by a variant principle of back-propagation, so that it represented what kinds of emotions can be identified from this movement.

    1. Swarm Behaviour Modelling based on Spiking Neural Network
    The general behaviours of a group of animals, such as ﬂock of birds or a bank of ﬁshes, follow some rules which may result from evolution or other adaptation for environment. The aim of this project is in what circumstances they change such behaviors. The key idea in flock of animals' behavior control is to provide sensory information (distance between fellow animals) as stimulus. We use spiking neural networks as one model to explain how individual decisions change the group behaviors, in which neural spikes to control movements of the animals and to decode the correspondant behavior.

    (Master Thesis) Bio-inspired Particle Filter and its SLAM application 
    The utilization of multi-robot systems has a major advantage when comparing to single robot systems, for example, with multiple robots working together, it has the potential to accomplish a task faster than a single robot. However, when a team of robots is sharing the same worksite, the Simultaneously Localization and Mapping (SLAM) problem becomes much more difficult to resolve because a huge amount of information is needed to be processed as well as analyzed. But on the other hand, multi-robot SLAM can be more efficient if robots can exchange and share information regarding their sensed data properly. In the SLAM problem, especially for Autonomous Underwater Vehicle (AUV) and Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV), it is necessary to include non-linear and non-Gaussian parameters, for which the traditional Kalman Filter (KF) cannot yield ideal solution. In applications involving non-linear and non-Gaussian parameters, Particle Filters (PF), which are based on the concept of Monte Carlo simulation, are more suitable estimation techniques. However, in problems involving multiple dimensions, such as the multi-robot SLAM problem, when a huge number of particles are being used, two problems, namely particle impoverishment and sample size dependency, will occur during the particle updating stage and these problems will become more severe. The problems will reduce the accuracy of the estimation results and resampling algorithms, such as Sequential Importance Sampling, Stratified Resampling and Systematic Resampling are used to alleviate these two problems.

    In my master thesis, a novel PF algorithm for tackling the particle impoverishment and sample size dependency problems is being studied and its application in a multi-robot system is examined. In this algorithm, Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) is incorporated into the generic particle filter in order to drive the proposal distribution to approximate the optimal solution. Mathematical proof and results obtained from a single variable estimation problem as well as from the robot localization problem show that, after the ACO optimization, better proposal distribution and more accurate estimation results can be obtained. In order to evaluate the performance of the ACO improved PF (PFACO) when applied to non-linear and non-Gaussian problems, such as the localization and SLAM problem, studies were conducted and utilization of PFACO algorithm for multi-robot systems was introduced. In a multi-robot environment, when two robots encounter, the same information on the same estimation problem represented by the two sets of particles will be re-evaluated based on information conveyed by particles from different sets. The particles are then merged into a single set and in such cases, parallel computing can be applied in order to reduce the processing time. By software simulation, our results are better than those from traditional approaches both in estimation error and execution time.
